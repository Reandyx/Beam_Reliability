Probabilistic Reliability Analysis and Reliability-Based Design of a Cantilever Beam under Material and Load Uncertainty

Structural systems operate under significant uncertainty in both material properties and applied loads, making probabilistic reliability analysis essential for safe and efficient design. This study develops a computational framework for evaluating the reliability of a cantilever beam subjected to uncertainty in Young’s modulus and external loading. The structural response is modeled using classical beam theory, with failure defined by exceeding an allowable deflection limit. Failure probability is evaluated using Monte Carlo simulation, the First-Order Reliability Method (FORM), and the Second-Order Reliability Method (SORM). The Hasofer–Lind–Rackwitz–Fiessler algorithm is employed to determine the most probable point of failure in standard normal space. To improve the efficiency of rare-event probability estimation, importance sampling and adaptive importance sampling techniques are implemented and compared with conventional Monte Carlo simulation. Sensitivity of the structural response to uncertain parameters is investigated using both FORM importance factors and global Sobol sensitivity indices. The results indicate that load variability dominates the variance of beam deflection, while material stiffness plays an important role in determining the reliability index near the failure boundary. The accuracy of FORM predictions is verified across a broad uncertainty domain and shown to agree closely with Monte Carlo estimates. Finally, a reliability-based design procedure is applied to determine the minimum beam height required to achieve a target reliability index. The developed framework demonstrates how probabilistic methods can provide deeper insight into structural performance while enabling more efficient engineering design under uncertainty.
